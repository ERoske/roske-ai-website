I made it to Everest Base Camp on April 15. So did Dawn. We took photos at the famous rock, we stood at 17,598 feet, and the next morning we climbed Kala Pattar to 18,570 feet for views that looked almost fake. Like Walt Disney himself had designed them.

That part you already know if you read [the prep post](/blog/everest-base-camp-preparation/). The part you don't know is how it actually happens, and specifically who makes it happen, because the answer is not "me."

## The cold is worse than the altitude

I told everyone I was worried about the altitude. I should have been worried about the cold.

We hiked through real weather. One day in the rain. One day in the snow. The walking-cold was fine. It was the sleeping-cold that nearly broke me. The teahouses (lodges along the trail) heat the dining room until everyone goes to bed, and then they let the bedrooms drop to whatever the Himalayas feel like that night. Which is "low."

Mummy sleeping bags assume you sleep on your back. I sleep on my back. They also wrap your face and chest tightly enough that, combined with thin air at 14,000 feet, the body interprets the whole arrangement as "something heavy is sitting on you, you cannot breathe, wake up now." This is not a relaxing way to be woken up. I had this experience nightly for about ten days.

The altitude is mostly a daytime problem. The cold is a nighttime problem. The nighttime problem wins.

There is also a smaller, stupider hardship nobody warns you about: the trail does not go up in a straight line. You climb 800 feet, feel triumphant, and then descend 400 feet to cross a river. Now you are 400 feet lower than you were thirty minutes ago. The math is correct. The morale is not.

## Diamox is a wonder drug, mostly

[Diamox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetazolamide), or acetazolamide, is the standard altitude medication. It tells your body to ventilate more aggressively, which keeps your blood oxygen up. I started taking it on day two. Five hikers in our group: four of us were on Diamox by the end. (Bob, the lone holdout, made it without. I would like to know who Bob is in regular life. Bob, if you are reading this, please write a memoir.)

In a teahouse one morning, an older Indian couple at our table mentioned the husband was not feeling well. I gave them my eloquent verbal commercial for the drug: "Feeling altitude sour? Try Diamox power!" They went off to the village clinic to get a prescription. Dawn and I walked into the main part of Dingboche to get massages, which is a thing you can apparently do at 14,500 feet, and is amazing and very odd. I'll allow it.

When we got back, only the wife was at the lodge. We asked where her husband was. She told us he had been emergency-evacuated by helicopter. The clinic had checked his blood oxygen and it was in the 50s, percentage-wise. (For reference, "fine" is 90 and up. 50 is "you should be in a hospital, not a teahouse.")

Altitude turns on you fast. The whole trek is a slow conversation with how much oxygen you have left, and your only real moves are go down or take the drug.

## Some people you meet on the trail

The Khumbu Valley is busy in April. There is a tea-lodge in Dingboche where Dawn and I happened to share a dining room with [Kristin Harila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristin_Harila), the Norwegian climber who summited [all fourteen of the world's 8,000-meter peaks](https://www.outsideonline.com/outdoor-adventure/everest/kristin-harila-tenjin-sherpa-8000-meter-peaks-14-speed-record/) in 92 days, breaking the previous record (six months and six days, set by a man) by more than half.

She barely comes up to my shoulder. She is kind, easy to talk to, and absolutely amazing. Her team stopped at the same teahouses we did, even though her hiking speed during the day was at least double ours. We saw her over several days. I have a new climber crush. (I asked. We got a photo. We are on a first-name basis. Yes, I am framing this.)

![Edward standing next to mountaineer Kristin Harila at a teahouse in Dingboche](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/with-kristin-harila.jpg)

A different night, we shared a lodge with the actress [Lily James](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cliffhanger_(2026_film)), who is now apparently a serious trekker because she fell in love with the sport while filming the Cliffhanger reboot. Which is a sentence I never expected to write.

## The 400-foot problem

The hardest moment of the trek was not Base Camp. Base Camp is at 17,598 feet, and we got there. The hardest moment was on Kala Pattar, four hundred feet from the top, with Dawn sitting down on a rock.

Some context. Dawn really did not want to do this trip. She is an artist. She paints in oils and watercolors. "High-altitude hiking in Nepal" was approximately tied with "extreme couponing" on her list of natural interests. She came because we wanted to build a memory together, and Dawn is a trooper.

Two weeks of acclimatization in Santa Fe and three months of stairwell work later, we were both in the best shape of our adult lives. (I used to run track, I lettered in wrestling, I am 51, and I have never been this fit. Make of that what you will.) Dawn outpaced me on several climbs and did not say a word about it.

But four hundred feet below the top of Kala Pattar, in thin air, she ran out. She sat down on a rock and told me to go on without her.

Here is what I learned in that moment: I could not. Not because the rules said so. Because going up with Dawn was the only thing keeping me going. I had been climbing for her. The solo finish to the summit was suddenly a lonely concept I had no interest in pursuing.

Our guide, Mike Roberts (who has summited the actual Everest seven times, and is older than both of us), sat down next to her. He gave her one of the quietest, most genuinely inspiring pep talks I have ever heard. He told her to rest. We did.

Then we went up the last four hundred feet together. The views from the top of Kala Pattar are the views I will remember for the rest of my life. After that we walked back down to Gorak Shep, then we walked to Everest Base Camp. Dawn did all of it.

![The view from the top of Kala Pattar at 18,570 feet, with the Himalayas behind](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/kala-pattar.jpg)

## The "photo rock" and the pile of rocks

Everest Base Camp is two different places.

The first place is the famous "photo rock," the one with the painted lettering and the prayer flags, the one in every photo you have ever seen of someone smiling at EBC. Arriving there is euphoric. We did it. Dawn and I will remember that moment forever.

![Edward and Dawn at the famous Everest Base Camp photo rock, prayer flags behind them](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/hero-photo-rock.jpg)

The second place is Everest Base Camp itself, which is a sprawl of dirty rocks, dirt, and tents about a mile long, sitting on top of an ice-cold glacier. (You do not see the glacier in any of the photos. It is, generously, the floor.) Our camp was on the far side. By the time we got to our tents, the euphoria had worn off and the only ambitions left were hot tea, more clothing, and sleep.

![Everest Base Camp itself: a mile-long sprawl of tents, rocks, and dirt sitting on a glacier](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/ebc-sprawl.jpg)

If you want a metaphor for the relationship between expectations and reality, "photo rock and then pile of rocks" is the cleanest one I have.

## The other half of the math

The trek was hard. It was also possible. The reason it was possible was the people.

Dawn was the reason I kept walking. I have already covered that.

The other reason was the porters. There were three of them carrying our overnight gear. Two were sixteen years old and the third was seventy. They carried everything on a strap around their foreheads, the traditional way, and they ran up trails I was crawling up. Edmund Hillary gets ninety percent of the credit. Tenzing Norgay gets ten. That math has always been wrong. Whenever I considered giving up, I would remember that two teenagers and a senior citizen were carrying everything I needed for the night while I was struggling with a day pack. It is hard to be the slowest middle-aged man on a mountain when the people doing the real work are not even a little tired.

![A Nepali porter carrying overnight gear up the trail with the traditional strap around the forehead](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/porters.jpg)

I tipped them well. (Dear goddess, they deserved it.)

## What I actually learned

Nobody summits anything alone, even when they technically summit alone. The mountain is a public collaboration between you, your partner, your guide, your porters, the people in the next lodge, the chemist who invented Diamox, and the strangers at the dining table who happen to be doing something incredible.

![The full support team of guides, cooks, and porters who actually made the trek possible](/images/blog/everest-base-camp-summit/massive-support-team.jpg)

Edmund Hillary always credited Tenzing Norgay. Nobody publishes a list of credits when they post the photo at the rock.

I think it works that way for most hard things, including the work I get paid for. I run AI companies in a wave of attention pointed mostly at the front of the room. The companies run because a lot of people do a lot of work that nobody photographs.

My father used to say "no one can take your experiences." (He was not specifically thinking of high-altitude Himalayan trekking when he said it, but the principle holds.) I will be carrying Kala Pattar and the photo rock and the four-hundred-foot rest and the porters and the teahouse helicopter and Kristin Harila for the rest of my life.

The next summit is undecided. Dawn votes for somewhere with heat in the rooms. I am inclined to agree.
