The market is voting on whether AI did the layoffs, and the votes are coming back surprisingly readable.

[CNBC tracked 23 S&P 500 firms that blamed AI for their layoffs](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/17/ai-related-layoffs-a-boost-for-stocks-not-necessarily.html). Fifty-six percent are trading in the red. Salesforce, which fired four thousand customer support roles and credited AI, is down 32 percent since the announcement. Nike is down 35. Fiverr is down 54.

[Cisco cut almost the same number of employees in the same week](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/13/cisco-csco-q3-earnings-report-2026.html), announced nine billion dollars in AI hyperscaler orders alongside the cut, and watched its stock jump 15 percent. Same headcount move, opposite market reaction.

What was different wasn't the layoff. It was the story.

## Three honest reasons companies do layoffs

Companies fire people for three reasons that have nothing to do with AI.

Growth slowed. Demand softened, the pipeline shrank, and the headcount no longer matches the work. This is the most common reason in any year, including this one.

A bet went wrong. A new product line missed, an acquisition didn't integrate, a region underperformed. The cut is the restructuring around the mistake.

The org chart aged out. The company that worked at 500 people doesn't work at 5,000. The cut is the second draft of the company.

None of these is "AI did the work." All three of them currently get AI dressed onto them in press releases, because AI sounds like the future and "growth slowed" sounds like a quarterly miss.

## What AI is actually replacing right now

Some jobs really are disappearing into AI in 2026. They are real, they are measurable, and they are not the jobs in most of the layoff announcements.

**HR**: Level 1 recruiting. Resume screening, initial candidate outreach, interview scheduling, and most pipeline triage are now done by agents. The recruiter picks up at the second-round and the offer stage.

**Customer Service**: Tier 1 support. Password resets, order status, return intake, FAQ-shaped tickets. Around half of customer interactions at companies that have actually deployed it. The de-escalations and the retention saves still belong to people.

**Finance and Accounting**: Accounts payable. Invoice ingestion, three-way match, exception routing, vendor inquiries. The touchless invoice rate at well-deployed shops has moved from single digits to north of 60 percent in two years. The controller and the FP&A team are not on that list.

**Sales Development**: Outbound sequences and lead enrichment. The SDR's first two hundred touches a day are now mostly generated, with a human stepping in once a prospect actually replies. The closers are not affected.

**Marketing**: Generic copy production. Product descriptions, A/B test variants, social posts, and most first drafts of newsletters. The brand voice, the campaign concept, and the launch strategy still come from humans.

**IT Help Desk**: Password resets, software provisioning, and the first pass on common tickets. The senior systems engineer holding the production database together at 3 AM is fine.

**Legal**: Standard contract review, NDA generation, and clause-by-clause compliance scans. The lawyer negotiating the actual deal is still negotiating the actual deal.

**Procurement**: Purchase order processing, vendor enrichment, RFP intake, and basic spend analytics. The strategic sourcing leader picking the contract is still picking the contract.

**Engineering**: Code completion, test scaffolding, documentation drafts, and most boilerplate. The architects designing the system are still designing the system, with an editor that finishes their thoughts faster.

Notice the pattern. The jobs AI is actually doing are first-pass, repetitive, and high-volume. The job titles in the layoff press releases (senior engineer, regional sales manager, head of strategy, VP of customer success) are almost never on the list. The titles don't match the technology. Somebody is dressing.

## Cisco vs. Salesforce was a natural experiment

Same week, same headcount cut, opposite stock reaction. As clean a comparison as the market ever gives us.

[Cisco told a revenue story](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/13/cisco-csco-q3-earnings-report-2026.html). AI hyperscaler orders just doubled. We are rotating headcount from the slowing categories into the growing ones. The cut is the price of growth, not the substitute for it.

[Salesforce told a cover story](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/17/ai-related-layoffs-a-boost-for-stocks-not-necessarily.html). AI agents now handle half of customer interactions, so we can cut four thousand customer support roles. The cut is the AI productivity win.

Both stories had AI in them. One had cash in it. Investors priced the two stories the way investors usually price stories. The one with the cash showing got rewarded. The one with the costume got marked down.

## The cost to your remaining people

When you announce that AI took someone's job, you are also announcing it to everyone whose job you didn't take.

Your survivors hear: the AI is coming for you next. Whether the AI is actually coming for them or not, they hear it. Adoption of the new tools slows because nobody wants to help the thing that's hunting them. Retention craters in the high-skill roles you actually need to keep. The number you saved by laying off four thousand people gets eaten by the number you spend re-recruiting eighteen months from now.

I have watched a finance team's AI rollout die because the rollout immediately followed an AI-themed layoff in the same building. The technology was fine. The story killed the deployment.

## The cost to your former people

"We laid you off because the AI got better" is a worse goodbye than "we laid you off because the business slowed."

One is a technological inevitability talk. The other is a human conversation. Former employees become former customers, former referrers, and former boomerang hires. The framing decides which list they end up on.

The honest version costs the CEO five minutes of discomfort. The dishonest version costs the company a chunk of its ambassador network for the next decade.

## The honest CEO playbook

If AI actually did the work, name the work and the number. "Our touchless invoice rate is 62 percent, which is why the AP team is going from 40 people to 20." That is a sentence the market understands and rewards.

If the business slowed, say so. "Our growth is now 11 percent instead of the 22 percent the headcount plan assumed. We are rightsizing." Also a sentence the market understands and rewards.

If a bet went wrong, own it. "We invested in the wrong region last year and we are restructuring around what worked." Same deal.

What the market does not reward is the dressing-up. "We are leveraging AI to drive operational efficiency" is the giveaway phrase. If you are using the word "leverage" as a verb, you are not having a real conversation, and the market is not having one with you either.

## What I actually believe about all of this

I'm a libertarian. I think the law should enforce exactly two things. Do everything you said you were going to do. And do not encroach on other people or their property. Everything else, the market and the courts can sort out.

So companies should be free to fire whenever they need to fire. A boss has no obligation to keep a job that the business doesn't need. That is the deal. That has always been the deal. I don't want the government writing rules about layoff justifications, AI-related or otherwise.

What I want is the second half of the contract.

If you are firing because AI did the work, prove it. Publish the touchless invoice rate, the tier-one deflection rate, the SDR pipeline coverage per rep. Let the market and your remaining employees look at the same number and agree. If the number is real, you are going to enjoy the stock pop Cisco got. Congratulations.

If you cannot prove it, you should not say it. Plenty of companies are lying right now, and the market is not gullible. The market is in the business of catching the dressing-up, and 32 percent down is what catching the dressing-up looks like.

The free market does the accountability work here without needing a single new regulation. We just need executives to stop helping themselves into the wrong column. Fire when you need to fire. Say what you actually did. Let the people who left walk out the door with their dignity. Let the people who stayed believe what you tell them. And if the AI actually did the work, prove it on the way out, the same way you would prove anything else.

That isn't a regulation. That's just keeping the first half of the contract.
